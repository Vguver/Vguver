## About me

From an early age, I was drawn to computing and electronics and took several related courses. Life eventually took me into a different branch of engineering, but Linux brought me back to that original curiosity.

A few years ago, after discovering creators like Chris Titus Tech and others in the Linux community, I started exploring Linux seriously in my spare time. Most of what I know has come from self-learning, experimentation, and more rebuilding, testing, and refining than I can count.

I am not a traditional software developer, and I do not come from a formal IT background. I am an enthusiast who learns by doing, studies how other people build their systems, and uses modern tools to turn useful ideas into practical projects.

---

## Why this GitHub exists

This GitHub is where I share projects designed to make Linux easier to understand, easier to experiment with, and easier to customize.

A big part of that motivation comes from helping family members replicate useful setups, understand how Linux desktops are structured, and choose how they want their own computers to behave. I want these projects to be practical first: useful for my own workflow, useful for the people around me, and hopefully useful for others as well.

## Featured project — isolated-desktops

[isolated-desktops](https://github.com/Vguver/isolated-desktops) is a session-profile manager for testing multiple Linux desktop setups on a single machine while keeping separate session homes and a cleaner workflow for analysis, installation, verification, and iteration.

It is built for people who want to:

- test multiple desktop environments without mixing user configuration
- understand how other Linux setups are structured
- compare approaches before committing to one
- edit and refine real profile files in a safer way
- learn through experimentation instead of guesswork

## How I approach customization

I spend a lot of time studying how others structure their desktops, dotfiles, and workflows, then adapting the parts that are genuinely useful. My goal is not to copy setups blindly, but to understand the logic behind them and build something cleaner, more reusable, and more personal.

I am especially interested in making Linux customization feel less overwhelming, so that new users can experiment with more confidence and gradually shape a desktop that fits the way they actually work.
